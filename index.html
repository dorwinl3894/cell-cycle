üåº Interphase üåº

Interphase, also known as replication, is the first phase of the cell cycle.
About 80% of a cell's lifespan is spent in this stage to replicate organelles for duplication.
Because this phase is so long, it is separated into 3 stages known as G1 Phase , S Phase, and G2 Phase.

G1 Phase

This is the first stage of Interphase. The cell makes a variety of proteins that are needed for DNA replication. The cell will only enter the next stage if there are sufficient growth factor signals telling the cell to progress.

S Phase

Also known as snythesis, this is the second stage of Interphase. DNA in the cell is packaged into chromosomes which is then replicated. As a result of replication, each chromosome now has two sister chromatids!

G2 Phase

This is the last stage of Interphase. The cell checks to make sure that DNA replication has been successfuly completed and if not, it will make any repairs needed. This cell also performs enzyme and protein synthesis as well as produce large amounts of ATP. This phase is the cell's final chance for the cell to grow before it splits!

üåª Prophase üåª

What happens in Prophase?

Prophase is considered the first true stage of the cell cycle process because of the major changes that occur in the cell. First, the cell beigns breaking down and creating structures. Chromosomes are condensed, which in turn makes it visible with microscopes. The nuclear envelope breaks down and the nucleolus will disappear from the cell.

Why is Prophase important?

A structure known as the mitotic spindle forms on the edges of the dividing cell. This structure is necessary to equally divide the chromosomes into two daughter cells during mitosis.

üåπ Metaphase üåπ

What happens in Metaphase?

In the very beginning of this stage, the pairs of condensed chromosomes line up along the equator of the cell. The nuclear membrane in the cell will also disappear completely, making it nucleus-free. After the removal of the membrane, chromosomes move to the "Metaphase Plate" which is basically chromosomes aligning at a right angle to the spindle poles.

üå∫ Anaphase üå∫

General Information

Anaphase is the fourth stage of mitosis, as well as the shortest stage. In this process, duplicated genetic materials, DNA packaged into chromosomes, are separated into opposite poles of the cell.

\

Disjunction

Disjunction in Anaphase is the process of spindle fibers shortening and sister chromatids being pulled apart at the centromere. As spindle fibers continue to move to opposite ends of the cell, chromosomes develop a distinguished V shape.

Telophase

This is the final stage of the cell cycle. This process separates the duplicated genetic material into their own nucleus, creating two nuclei in one cell. Afterwards, the kinetochore microtubules that pulled the duplicated material into opposite ends of the cell begin to dissolve, but the cell continues to elongate.

Cytokinesis

Cytokinesis is the second part of Telophase, which is responsible for being the process in which the cell will divide into two. Chromosomes begin to uncoil, which makes them diffuse and less compact. To seperate into two cells, it pinches itself in the middle until it fully cuts off. After that, the cell cycle will repeat the whole process again!

‚õî Cell Regulation ‚õî

Cell regulation happens throughout the cell cycle, but one important times that cell regulation really needs to be working is in the Interphase stage, G1 phase.

G1 Phase

The cell will move to S phase only if there is enough growth factor signals telling the cell to progress. If cells do not get enough necessary signals , it will move into G0 phase. The cell will be in an inactive state , meaning they will stop preparing to divide.



Some growth factor signals include ...


  • Is the DNA in the cell damaged?
    • If YES, then repair the cell.
    • If NO, then continue Interphase.

  • Has the cell grown sufficiently?
    • If YES, then stop growing.
    • If NO, then continue growing.

Cancer Cells

Q: What happens if the cell stops responding to any signals given, even signals that control cellular growth and death?


A: The cell will continue to grow and become a Cancer Cell. These cells will grow and divide quickly, break through tissue boundaries and spread to different areas in the body, and can not be destroyed by the process of programmed cell death, which is a self destruction of a cell.



Q: Why might a cell stop responding to signals it is given?

A: Normal cells during Interphase have hundreds of genes controlling the process of cell division. The average cell requires a balance of genes that promote cell growth and genes that suppress cell growth. Unfortunately, mutations are able to occur in these genes. For cancer cells specifically, genes that control cell growth is mutated, making it unable to create strong signals to stop the cell from growing.


üå± Mitosis vs Meiosis üå±

Whats the difference?

Mitosis and Meiosis go through the same stages of the cell cycle (Interphase, Prophase, Metaphase, anaphase, Telephase) but are they are very different from each other. The difference is that meiosis creates four daughter cells instead of two in mitosis.

Four daughter cells are able to be created because meiosis happens two times known as meiosis I and meiosis II. Each daughter cell also contains unique combinations of genetic material, which will combine to make genetic variations of gametes.

QA

Q: Why do the daughter cells only have half the number of chromosomes as the parenet cell?

A: This is because meiosis creates cells that will become gametes (also known as reproductive cells) to make zygotes, which will ultimately become a baby!



Q: How does Meiosis I differ from Meiosis II?

A: Meiosis II begins with either interkinesis or interphase II. Meiosis II will not have S phase in Interphase because DNA is already replicated in Meiosis I. Each stage of the cell cycle in Meiosis II will differ slighty from Meiosis I.
